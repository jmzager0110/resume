Getting Started in Automation of Quality

or Any Other Manner of Software Quality Assurance

Know Your Role

Docs to Look For

While you're not always going to be able to participate in "shift-left" testing, there are common practices on any software development team, like documentation. Here's some key items to look for:

  • Team practice methodology docs, like sprint cycles, workflow requirements, definition of done
  • Tech stack architecture or structure
  • Responsibilities of your department, including a Test Plan, so you know exactly what's out of scope or "hands-off"
  • Analytics, specifically supported devices, browsers, & viewports
  • Instructions for different testing flows, like what tools are used for testing accessibility or link validation
  • Existing automated testing suite(s), any CI/CD implementation
  • Obviously key items in the ticketing software, like test case repository, and templates for bug tickets, etc.

Get Your Feet Wet

Once you understand your responsibilities, including your expected workflow, AND you have your computer configured correctly, dig in! What, I didn't mention anything about computer set up? Ah, well...

For Manual Testing

  • What are you allowed to use for mobile device testing?
  • What are you required to use for accessibility testing?
  • Are there any licensed/third party tools you need access to?
  • Be mindful of different desktop browsers having different accessibility settings, looking at you, Safari.
  • Do you have a good screenshot/screen recording tool already installed?
  • Are there any privacy restrictions, like VPN connection requirements?

For Automated Testing

  • Do you have access to clone the repository?
  • What's the tech stack required? Try to install all the technologies to ensure there's no blockers.
  • Where that CI/CD happen? Do you have access?
  • Review the existing practices, like naming conventions, organization, tags & annotations.

That's the hard part, the tech stuff! Once you've managed to get a handle on all of that, take a breath! You're ready!

Set Yourself Up for The Long Haul

Be Selfish with Your Time

You might be incredibly eager to spend your down time on getting better, hitting deadlines, or building something of your own. Keep in mind, you can't perform quality work if you always burn the candle at both ends. You run the risk of getting burned out in many ways, but specifically, don't make yourself grow to shudder at the mention of technology. You're probably not saving lives, & unpaid work is just that. Also, in a lot of roles, unreported hours means unrealistic expectations when the budget is confirmed for the next year.

Listen to Yourself

Technology evolves at the speed of light, & so sometimes, your team's needs will as well. You maybe excited at the concept of jumping into a new role, like engineer or lead, but check yourself before you wreck yourself. What comes with this position change? Are your responsibilities outlined? Will they fit within the hours wou're able to work? Finally, when considering a leadership role, how comfortable are you with confrontation? What is within your rights in that role? How will you handle conflict, be it with a report, or a senior lead? Even the client?
